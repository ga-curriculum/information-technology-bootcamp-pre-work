## Clear communication

As an IT professional, you'll need to communicate with a wide range of people, from people on your team to *stakeholders*, clients, and end-users. Some of those individuals won't have technical backgrounds, so you must be able to explain complex technical concepts in a way they can understand.

> 📚 *Stakeholders* are individuals or groups who have an interest in the outcome of a project. They can include people in your organization, such as team members, other teams, managers, or leadership, and individuals outside your organization, such as clients or partners.

You'll need to be able to ask the right questions to gather information and to listen carefully to the answers. You'll also need to write clearly and concisely and document your work so others can understand it. These are skills that you can develop over time, but start with these basics:

- **Frequent check-ins**: Regularly check in with your stakeholders to ensure everyone is aligned on the project's goals and progress. This ensures that if you need to make changes, they can be made early while the problem is still small.
- **Default to open**: Promote transparency and trust with your colleagues by defaulting to open communication. Share progress, challenges, and successes with your team to foster a culture of collaboration and ensure everyone is on the same page.
- **Get input**: Ensure that you're getting input from all stakeholders, not just the ones who are the loudest or most visible. This can help you avoid missing essential requirements.
- **Confirm large work requirements**: When given a large task that will take significant time, confirm the requirements with your stakeholders in writing before you start. This can help you avoid misunderstandings and ensure you're working on the right thing.
- **Speak up when someone uses terms or acronyms you're unfamiliar with**: If someone uses a term you're not familiar with, ask them to explain it. The potential embarrassment of not knowing a term is far less than the potential embarrassment caused by misunderstanding something and not speaking up in the moment. Most of the time, people will be happy to explain a term to you and won't think any less of you for asking.

> 🧠 Note that most of the above require you to get clear communication from other people to ensure you're doing the right task. Observe how others communicate and the patterns they use that help them communicate. Emulate the things that work and avoid those that don't in your communication.

## Confidently communicating as an IT professional

As a new IT professional, you may feel like you don't know enough to communicate confidently. This can cause those new to the field to feel *imposter syndrome*.

> 📚 *Imposter syndrome* occurs when an individual doubts their accomplishments and has a persistent or occasional internalized fear of being exposed as a fraud, even though they're not.

These feelings are normal. It's important to remember that you're not expected to know everything and that it's okay not to know something.

If you ask a mentor in the industry, they will likely tell you that they started their career with the same feelings of doubt and uncertainty and were faking it until they made it. Then, one day, they realized they weren't faking it anymore.

Often, when you need to communicate about an issue or a project, you must persuade others to make the correct choices confidently. So, how can you also fake it until you make it? Well, the secret is that you don't have to fake it at all. You can start communicating confidently by:

- **Being prepared**: If you're discussing a project or an issue, ensure you're prepared. Have the information you need in front of you, and be ready to answer questions.
- **Anticipating questions**: Consider the questions that might be asked and have answers prepared.
- **Being honest**: If you don't know something, say so. It's better to admit you don't know something and suggest a follow-up conversation after researching than pretending you know something and make a mistake. You have no greater professional asset than people's trust in you. Never risk that trust.
- **Practicing**: The more you practice communicating, the more confident you'll become. Practice explaining technical concepts to someone who doesn't have a technical background. Practice writing clear and concise emails. Practice documenting your work. The more you practice, the more confident you'll become in your skills. Use this course as an opportunity to practice every day!

Confidence has real and concrete power. It can help you amplify your voice and message and help build your influence and impact.
