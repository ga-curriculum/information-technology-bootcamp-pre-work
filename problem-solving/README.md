## Solving problems for real people

At their core, IT professionals are problem solvers. However, those problems are typically brought to them by people who don't have the technical knowledge to solve them on their own.

Working with others to understand their problems and find solutions is a key part of an IT professional's job. Successfully managing these relationships is just as important as your technical skills.

Before you dive into solving technical problems, it's important to take a step back and understand what you're trying to solve. Do your best to understand the issue from the perspective of the person who brought it to you. Put another way, practice empathy to help foster trust. When a person presents you with a problem, start by:

- **Listening carefully**: Avoid distractions and pay attention to what the person is saying. Ask follow-up questions to clarify anything that's unclear.
- **Being patient**: Solving problems can take time. Be patient with the person you're helping. Remember that they're likely feeling frustrated or overwhelmed, and your patience can help put them at ease.
- **Understanding motivations**: What is the person trying to accomplish? It may sound surprising, but people often skip their core motivations behind asking for your help.

  For example, suppose someone comes to you because they want to install an application your organization typically wouldn't support. In that case, you should understand why they're trying to install it in the first place. Maybe you can solve the problem without needing to install the application at all. Understanding the motivations behind the problem can help you find a solution that meets their needs.

Finish by summarizing the problem back to the person in your own words. This will ensure that you've understood the problem correctly and give the person a chance to clarify anything you might have missed or recall additional details that could be helpful.

> 📚 In a business help-desk environment, it is common to refer to an individual who comes to you to solve a technical issue as an *end-user* or *customer*, even if you both work for the same organization. You may also hear the more precise term, *internal customer*.

## Solving technical problems

Once you understand the problem, you can start working on a solution. This course will teach you many technical skills, but solving technical problems is often more about your approach than your technical knowledge.

Here are some general steps you can take to solve technical problems:

1. **Identify the problem**: This started with your initial conversation with the end-user but might also involve some initial investigation of your own to help you better understand the situation.
2. **Research the problem**: This might involve searching online, using an AI assistant, reading documentation, asking for help from others, or just utilizing your existing knowledge if you're already familiar with the issue.
3. **Test a solution**: Once you have a few possible solutions, start testing them individually, beginning with the fix you think will most likely solve the problem.
4. **Evaluate the result**: Once you've implemented a solution, evaluate the result. Did the problem get fixed? Then move on to the next step! If not, think through why, and use that data to inform your next test and return to step 3.
5. **Communicate the results**: Finally, communicate the results back to the end-user. Explain what you did to solve the problem, and make sure they understand what you did and why.

Throughout the process, document the steps you take and what you find. This will help you keep track of what you've tried and can be helpful if you need help from others. It can also help you communicate the results back to the end-user.

This is an example of a systematic procedure - a predefined set of steps you can follow to solve a problem or achieve a desired result. Systematic procedures involve breaking down a problem into clear and manageable steps and working through each one to find a solution. While procedures like this may seem rigid, they can be incredibly helpful when faced with a complex problem and minimize the risk of failure.

Engaging in this type of systematic thought is a key skill for IT professionals because it helps you approach problems in a structured way and can help you avoid missing necessary steps or making mistakes. It can also help you communicate your process to others, which is essential when working on a team or explaining your work to someone else.

<div class="activity solo-exercise">
  <h2 class="title">Identifying systematic processes</h2>
  <span class="minutes">10 min</span>
</div>

Think about a process you follow in your daily life involving several steps. It could be something as simple as making a cup of coffee or something more complex like planning a trip. Write down the steps you follow to complete that process as if you were explaining them to someone doing that for the first time. Here are some things to think about:

- How do you know when you've completed each step?
- Is there a possibility of failure at any point in the process? What should happen if something doesn't go as planned?
- How do you know when you've completed the entire process?
